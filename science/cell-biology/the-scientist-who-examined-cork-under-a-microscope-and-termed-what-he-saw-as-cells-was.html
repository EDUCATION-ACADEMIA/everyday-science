The scientist who examined cork under a microscope and termed what he saw as cells was

(A) Theodore Schwan

(B) Matthias Schlieden

(C) Rudolf Virchow

(D) ** Robert Hooke

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -Hooke detailed his observations of this tiny and previously unseen world in his book, Micrographia. To him, the cork looked as if it was made of tiny pores, which he came to call “cells” because they reminded him of the cells in a monastery.

Concept note-2: -In the 1660s, Robert Hooke looked through a primitive microscope at a thinly cut piece of cork. He saw a series of walled boxes that reminded him of the tiny rooms, or cellula, occupied by monks. Medical historian Dr. Howard Markel discusses Hooke’s coining of the word “cell."

Concept note-3: -Robert Hooke was the first scientist who observed cells in the cork of a tree and hence is regarded as the person who discovered cells. Q.

Concept note-4: -Robert Hooke, (born July 18 [July 28, New Style], 1635, Freshwater, Isle of Wight, England-died March 3, 1703, London), English physicist who discovered the law of elasticity, known as Hooke’s law, and who did research in a remarkable variety of fields.
